# 去身份化职场行为模式库

Created: 2026-05-25

This file synthesizes `source_notes.csv` into behavior patterns for later skill creation. Nationality and ethnicity terms are allowed in search recall, but they are not used as explanations in this taxonomy.

## Evidence Key

- Level A: supported by research, reputable management/tutorial sources, or multiple independent high-quality sources.
- Level B: repeated in forum/tutorial material, useful as a risk signal but not a statistical conclusion.
- Level C: single or highly biased examples; use only as prompts for further search or as examples of what to strip out.

## Core Distinction

Do not ask: "Which nationality does this?"

Ask:

1. What behavior is observable?
2. What incentive does it exploit?
3. What artifact proves or disproves it?
4. What countermeasure reduces ambiguity without creating unnecessary conflict?

## Level A Patterns

### 1. Proactive Upward Framing

Sources: S001, S002, S015

Behavior:

- Anticipates the boss's questions before being asked.
- Translates execution detail into business outcomes.
- Brings options and recommendations instead of only problems.
- Matches the leader's preferred communication format.

Mechanism:

Leadership attention is scarce. The person who turns work into decision-ready signals controls the management narrative.

Use:

- Strong for executive updates, weekly reports, resource requests, and weak-KPI explanations.

Countermove:

- If someone else owns the upward narrative around your work, publish your own concise update with facts, owner, artifact, risk, and ask.

### 2. Low-Context Outcome Framing

Sources: S004, S010, S014

Behavior:

- Leads with conclusion, status, measurable outcome, and decision needed.
- Makes ownership and next steps explicit.
- Uses short, scannable updates.

Mechanism:

In direct, task-oriented environments, ambiguity reads as lack of ownership. Visibility comes from concise status, measurable impact, and clear asks.

Use:

- Useful in US-style corporate settings, fast-moving engineering/product teams, and exec updates.

Countermove:

- Do not rely on hints. Ask: "What decision is needed, by when, and who owns it?"

### 3. Diplomatic Decoding

Sources: S005, S006, S007

Behavior:

- Disagreement or criticism is softened by polite, indirect, or coded language.
- Authority may be exercised through implication rather than direct command.

Mechanism:

Relationship-preserving language can hide decision status. Literal interpretation misses risk.

Use:

- Useful in British, diplomatic, consensus-heavy, or conflict-avoidant organizations.

Countermove:

- Restate politely: "To make sure I understood: are we aligned to proceed, or is this a concern we need to resolve first?"

### 4. Written Confirmation And Meeting Accountability

Sources: S003, S013, S014, S016, S017

Behavior:

- Converts meeting discussion into durable records: decisions, owners, deadlines, blockers, and risks.
- Backs up verbal agreement with written confirmation.

Mechanism:

Verbal ambiguity lets responsibility drift. Written records freeze decision state and make later blame shifting harder.

Use:

- Core defensive pattern for meetings, cross-team dependencies, matrix teams, external vendors, and remote work.

Countermove:

- Send a "to confirm" note after verbal decisions.
- Use a fixed table: Decision / Owner / Due date / Dependency / Risk / Next check.

### 5. Credit Overclaiming And Visibility Capture

Sources: S008, S009, S010, S011

Behavior:

- A person presents team or peer work as their own.
- A person who summarizes or reports upward captures disproportionate credit.
- Contribution boundaries are vague until recognition time.

Mechanism:

In collaborative work, contributions are often hard to attribute. The visible narrator can become the perceived owner.

Use:

- Core pattern for "抢功" detection and defense.

Countermove:

- Define roles before the work.
- Publish milestone updates while work is ongoing.
- Attach artifacts: design doc, commits, analysis, meeting notes, decision log.
- In a meeting, calmly add: "To complete the picture, my part was X, and Y owned Z."

### 6. Influence Without Authority

Sources: S027, S028, S029

Behavior:

- Wins resources or alignment without formal reporting power.
- Maps stakeholders, interests, approval gates, and currencies.
- Frames asks as tradeoffs, risks, or value exchanges.

Mechanism:

Matrix organizations distribute authority. People who understand process gates and stakeholder incentives can steer outcomes without title power.

Use:

- Resource requests, cross-functional work, blocked dependencies, roadmap negotiation.

Countermove:

- Ask who has decision rights, who benefits, who pays the cost, and which tradeoff leadership must approve.

### 7. Escalation As Decision Framing

Sources: S012, S014

Behavior:

- Raises a blocker only when it exceeds one's authority.
- States issue, impact, options, decision needed, and deadline.

Mechanism:

Escalation is not complaining. It transfers a tradeoff to the level that actually owns the decision.

Use:

- Dependency stalled, milestone at risk, resource conflict, scope conflict.

Countermove:

- Do not absorb an impossible tradeoff silently. Escalate with facts and options.

### 8. Impression-Management Portfolio

Sources: S041, S042, S044, S045

Behavior:

- Uses self-promotion, praise, opinion conformity, favor rendering, exemplification, or relationship work to shape evaluation.
- Presents diligence, loyalty, alignment, or helpfulness as visible career signals.

Mechanism:

Work evaluation is partly social. People who know which audience they are influencing can shape perceived performance beyond raw delivery.

Use:

- Core pattern for "只会说不会干", "会包装", "会向上管理", "会讨好老板" analysis.

Countermove:

- Do not attack the tactic directly. Ask what artifact, delivery result, business outcome, or decision evidence supports the claimed contribution.

### 9. Influence-Tactic Classification

Sources: S043, S048, S049, S077, S078

Behavior:

- Uses rational persuasion, consultation, coalition, pressure, exchange, legitimating, personal appeals, or inspirational appeal to win support.

Mechanism:

Resource and priority decisions are influenced through repeatable tactic families. Some are transparent and evidence-based; some are coercive or political.

Use:

- Resource grabs, priority fights, cross-functional asks, leadership buy-in.

Countermove:

- Name the tactic internally, then respond with the right demand: evidence for rational persuasion, tradeoff for exchange, decision-right clarity for coalition or pressure.

### 10. Bad-News Avoidance And Upward Distortion

Sources: S046, S047, S066, S067, S068, S069

Behavior:

- Delays, softens, or buries bad news.
- Reports only optimistic interpretations upward.
- Avoids clear owner, impact, or decision ask when a miss is likely.

Mechanism:

Hierarchy and punishment risk create silence. People protect themselves by keeping negative facts ambiguous until they are unavoidable.

Use:

- Project delays, weak KPIs, failed experiments, missed commitments.

Countermove:

- Escalate early with issue, impact, root cause confidence, action already taken, options, and decision needed.

### 11. Pre-Wire And Pre-Brief

Sources: S075, S076

Behavior:

- Aligns key stakeholders one-on-one before the formal decision meeting.
- Tests objections privately and adapts the proposal before the public forum.

Mechanism:

Many decisions are shaped before the official meeting. Public meetings often ratify or contest pre-built alignment.

Use:

- Resource requests, roadmap changes, controversial proposals, executive decisions.

Countermove:

- If surprised in a group meeting, ask to separate decision from first exposure: "This is new information for me; can we take one working session to assess impact before finalizing?"

### 12. Meeting Airtime And Process Control

Sources: S062, S063, S064, S065

Behavior:

- Dominates airtime, interrupts, derails, or repeatedly summarizes others' ideas.
- Uses agenda, timebox, parking lot, action items, and final summaries to control the meeting.

Mechanism:

Meeting memory attaches to who frames the issue, who gets airtime, and who writes the recap.

Use:

- 开会抢话, 会议控场, 被截流, 被压制.

Countermove:

- Use direct but calm scripts: name the interrupter, say you are not finished, return to the point, then send written recap with owner and contribution.

### 13. Action-Title Narrative Control

Sources: S083, S084, S085, S086, S087, S120, S121, S122

Behavior:

- Makes slide titles conclusions rather than topics.
- Turns a slide deck into a standalone decision artifact.
- Frames weak or mixed data through executive-relevant story flow.

Mechanism:

Executives often skim titles first. Whoever writes the title layer controls interpretation.

Use:

- PPT, QBR, board update, project recap, weak KPI reframe.

Countermove:

- If another person writes the title layer over your work, review whether titles match evidence and ask for contribution/owner notes where needed.

## Level B Patterns

### 14. Asymmetric Credit/Blame Funnel

Sources: S020, S021, S024, S025, S093, S095, S096, S097, S099

Behavior:

- Wins are reported upward by a manager or coordinator as their contribution.
- Failures are pushed downward to executors or dependency owners.

Mechanism:

Control of reporting channels lets a person route upside and downside differently.

Countermove:

- Keep work-in-progress visible.
- Confirm decisions and blockers before they become failures.
- Record who decided, who executed, and who accepted risk.

### 15. Matrix Crossfire

Sources: S022, S023, S026

Behavior:

- Multiple managers or stakeholders assign conflicting work.
- One person controls ratings while another controls delivery expectations.
- Goals shift without a record.

Mechanism:

Ambiguous authority lets each side externalize cost to the worker.

Countermove:

- Ask for one priority owner.
- Maintain a target-change log.
- Put tradeoffs in writing: "If A is priority, B moves to DATE unless extra support is assigned."

### 16. Summary-Right Capture

Sources: S019, S020, S111, S131, S134

Behavior:

- A person waits until the end of the meeting and summarizes others' ideas as their own position.
- Phrases like "this is what I meant" or "to summarize my proposal" can absorb prior contributions.

Mechanism:

Meeting memory often attaches to the last coherent summary, not the original speaker.

Countermove:

- Summarize your own contribution before the meeting closes.
- Send recap naming contributions and owners.
- Use neutral correction: "Yes, building on what I raised earlier, the proposal is..."

### 17. Goal-Shift Risk

Sources: S026, S017, S096

Behavior:

- Targets, criteria, or expectations change after work has started.
- Later evaluation ignores the original constraints.

Mechanism:

Without a baseline, later narratives can redefine success or failure.

Countermove:

- Preserve original target, change date, decision maker, reason, and impact.
- Confirm whether the change replaces or adds to the original target.

### 18. Language-Faultline Risk

Sources: S003, S034, S035

Behavior:

- Side conversations happen in a language not shared by all stakeholders.
- Decisions or clarifications occur outside the common working channel.

Mechanism:

Language subgroups create information asymmetry even when a corporate language exists.

Countermove:

- Require business decisions and blockers to be summarized in the shared working language.
- Avoid accusing people of exclusion; frame it as decision auditability.

### 19. Authority Calibration

Sources: S036, S037, S040, S123, S124

Behavior:

- A meeting appears to reach agreement, but attendees do not actually have decision authority.
- Decision-making depends on seniority, hierarchy, or off-stage approval.

Mechanism:

Meeting participation is not the same as commitment authority. Confusing the two creates false alignment.

Countermove:

- Ask: "Who can approve this decision, and what written confirmation do we need before execution?"

### 20. Record-Resistance Risk

Sources: S089, S090, S104, S105, S106, S107

Behavior:

- A stakeholder avoids writing decisions, rejects project systems, or keeps changes in verbal/side channels.

Mechanism:

Record avoidance preserves future flexibility and blame-shifting room.

Countermove:

- Maintain neutral logs anyway: meeting minutes, RAID log, issue log, decision log, and "please correct by date" emails.

### 21. Assistance Boundary Drift

Sources: S092, S104

Behavior:

- A person asks for "help", but later responsibility for the output or failure shifts to the helper.

Mechanism:

Unclear distinction between advice, assistance, review, and ownership lets responsibility migrate.

Countermove:

- Write: "I can advise/review X; final ownership and sign-off remain with Y."

### 22. Ill-Defined Goal Politics

Sources: S098, S126, S127, S129, S130

Behavior:

- Vague goals let people compete through visibility, proximity, and narrative instead of clear delivery.
- Performance is judged by who tells the story rather than what was agreed and delivered.

Mechanism:

Ambiguity creates political surface area.

Countermove:

- Clarify success criteria, baseline, decision rights, owner, evidence, and review date before execution.

### 23. Single-Source Status Defense

Sources: S088, S089, S108, S109, S110, S116, S117, S139

Behavior:

- Status is compiled from inconsistent verbal updates or undocumented assumptions.

Mechanism:

The person publishing status inherits blame if inputs are wrong and ownership is not recorded.

Countermove:

- Require written owner inputs for key status fields: progress, risk, blocker, decision needed, due date.

### 24. Root-Cause Reframing

Sources: S057, S103, S118

Behavior:

- Converts accusation or emotional blame into reconstruction of facts, handoffs, contributing factors, and prevention.

Mechanism:

Blame narrows attention to a scapegoat. Root-cause framing expands attention to system and decision points.

Countermove:

- Say: "Let's reconstruct the timeline and handoff points so we can fix the cause and prevent repeat."

### 25. Standup Status Capture

Sources: S136, S134

Behavior:

- A coworker asks what you are doing, then reports that progress first in standup or status channels.
- The visible speaker becomes associated with the work even if they did not do the core execution.

Mechanism:

Daily standup rewards first coherent status. If your work is visible only through another speaker, ownership can drift.

Countermove:

- Report your own status early and specifically.
- Mention artifacts and blockers.
- Keep dependency boundaries explicit: "My connector is ready; backend endpoint X is still pending owner Y."

### 26. Meeting-Minutes Control

Sources: S138, S141

Behavior:

- One person writes meeting minutes, chooses who is named, who is omitted, and which risks or owners are recorded.

Mechanism:

Minutes become the official memory. The note-taker can subtly assign credit, omit contribution, or create distance from failure.

Countermove:

- Rotate minute ownership for critical meetings.
- Ask for contributor/owner table.
- Reply with corrections by a clear deadline if minutes omit material facts.

### 27. Information Isolation

Sources: S141, S137

Behavior:

- Key information is withheld, side-channeled, not documented, or not CC'd to the person who needs it.
- A person is kept out of reports or decision threads so their contribution or context disappears.

Mechanism:

Information access shapes both execution power and perceived ownership.

Countermove:

- Create shared docs, decision logs, CC norms, and access criteria.
- Ask for written rationale when access is denied for "risk" reasons.

### 28. Visible-Project Selection

Sources: S142, S070, S071, S072

Behavior:

- People compete for high-visibility projects while avoiding low-visibility labor.
- Resource or opportunity requests are framed through risk, strategic priority, or visibility.

Mechanism:

Career systems reward visible impact more than invisible maintenance unless maintenance is explicitly reported.

Countermove:

- Make invisible labor visible.
- Publish operational load, risk reduction, and avoided incidents.
- Tie project assignment to transparent criteria.

## Level C / Bias-Only Inputs

Sources: S032, S033, S112, S115, S131, S132, S133, S134, S135, S137, S138, S141, S142

These sources contain identity-heavy claims and should not be used as evidence that a nationality or ethnicity behaves a certain way. They are useful only to identify public stereotype themes that need to be converted into testable behavior questions:

- Was a claim made without a named deliverable?
- Was a promise tracked against actual delivery?
- Did one person control the upward report?
- Were contributions mapped to artifacts?
- Did hiring, referrals, or networks bypass normal evaluation?

## Candidate Skill Functions

### Analyze Incident

Input: user's story, meeting note, email, or chat excerpt.

Output:

- observed behaviors;
- evidence strength;
- likely mechanism;
- risk to user;
- recommended written response;
- follow-up artifact to create.

### Rewrite Defensive Email

Input: messy situation.

Output:

- neutral "to confirm" email;
- decision/action table;
- risk and dependency section;
- ask and deadline.

### Prepare Offensive Reporting

Input: raw progress or weak KPI.

Output:

- executive headline;
- status and confidence;
- facts vs interpretation;
- action plan;
- resource or decision ask.

### Detect Narrative Capture

Input: meeting transcript or summary.

Output:

- who framed the problem;
- who summarized;
- who got action ownership;
- whose contribution was omitted;
- correction text.

## Guardrails For The Future Skill

- Do not infer behavior from nationality, ethnicity, race, gender, caste, religion, accent, or visa status.
- If user supplies identity labels, store them only as context and ask for observable behavior.
- Treat forum complaints as hypotheses, not facts.
- Prefer written artifacts over memory.
- Prefer role, owner, incentive, and decision rights over personality judgments.
- Never recommend discriminatory screening or identity-based retaliation.
