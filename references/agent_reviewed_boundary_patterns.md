# Agent-Reviewed Boundary Patterns

This public-safe reference distills an internal all-note agent review pass into
source-free workplace boundary mechanisms. It does not include raw notes, titles,
authors, screenshots, OCR text, quotes, paths, prompt traces, private scoring,
or internal pressure-testing components.

Use these patterns as qualitative design background, not as statistical proof
about any nationality, race, ethnicity, gender, accent, religion, caste,
immigration status, or other protected trait.

## Use Rules

- Diagnose observable artifacts and responsibility chains, not identities.
- Prefer concrete forums: meeting, email, status deck, decision log, tracker,
  review packet, promotion packet, client review, or recurring work record.
- Treat sales language, slogans, and broad career advice as noise unless they
  contain a concrete workplace artifact, owner route, decision state, deadline,
  or defensive action.
- Do not convert these mechanisms into manipulation, retaliation, exclusion, or
  blame-shifting tactics.

## Pattern Clusters

### 1. Record And Audience Control

Risk:
The visible record can decide who owns a result, who owns a blocker, and who
gets remembered. A changed CC list, missing recap, vague deck owner, or
uncorrected meeting minute can become the durable version of events.

Defensive translation:
Name the source-of-truth artifact, record owner, correction deadline,
same-audience update path, and fallback addendum path. Do not rely on memory
when a written record will harden.

### 2. Owner And Decision Ambiguity

Risk:
Vague subject wording can hide who proposed, decided, executed, approved,
blocked, accepted risk, or owns the next update. Ambiguity enables both credit
drift and blame drift.

Defensive translation:
Split owners by verb. Use an owner matrix when record ownership, decision
rights, evidence ownership, risk acceptance, and execution ownership are not the
same.

### 3. Meeting Airtime And Memory Hooks

Risk:
The room often remembers the most compact sentence, not the deepest work. Slow
entry, soft opening, or unprotected airtime can let another person become the
remembered speaker.

Defensive translation:
Prepare a one-line decision, risk, option, or recommendation before the meeting;
say it early; protect unfinished points calmly; then send a concise same-thread
recap.

### 4. Upward Decision Framing

Risk:
An exhaustive option menu can push decision cost upward and be read as avoiding
judgment. Leaders usually need recommendation, rationale, tradeoff, owner, and
next step.

Defensive translation:
Turn "what should we do?" into "my recommendation is X because Y; the tradeoff
is Z; if you disagree by D, I will proceed with A." Keep alternatives visible,
but do not make the manager do the user's analysis.

### 5. Visibility, Credit, And Promotion Evidence

Risk:
Good work without a memorable problem label, metric, audience route, or
contribution map can stay invisible. Credit can drift when the artifact names
the presenter but not the contributor.

Defensive translation:
Map contribution to artifact, artifact to business effect, and business effect
to the right audience. Preserve team credit while making the user's role
specific and evidenced.

### 6. Expectation And Deadline Anchoring

Risk:
Unanchored phrases such as "later", "will review", or "let's revisit" can
freeze progress while appearing polite. Missing deadline or follow-up owner
creates drift.

Defensive translation:
Attach state, next date, owner, and escalation threshold. Even if there is no
answer yet, the record should say when the next answer is due and who owns it.

### 7. Cross-Functional Leverage And Stakeholder Routes

Risk:
Low-power actors can be forced to chase teams that have no incentive or
authority to help. Direct asks fail when the recipient does not own the
tradeoff.

Defensive translation:
Ask whether the dependency owner has incentive and authority. If not, route the
ask through a shared business objective, decision right, or stakeholder whose
goal already contains the dependency.

### 8. Client, Bad News, And Risk Packaging

Risk:
Bad news buried as status, excuse, or blame can make the user look passive.

Defensive translation:
Name the business impact first, give options with tradeoffs, recommend one
path, identify the owner and needed decision, and record the next deadline.

### 9. Language And Pragmatic Signal Friction

Risk:
Language or tone issues matter when they change a concrete artifact: missed
action item, misunderstood deadline, vague approval, relationship temperature,
or unasked clarification.

Defensive translation:
Do not treat language anxiety as identity weakness. Convert it into confirmation
scripts, deadline checks, owner checks, recaps, and neutral clarification.

### 10. Low-Signal And Noise Handling

Risk:
Not every source contains workplace signal. Hashtags, sales copy, motivation
posts, and empty short videos can create false pattern confidence.

Defensive translation:
Extract only concrete workplace artifacts, phrases, decision states, audience
routes, or defensive moves. Mark noise as noise instead of overfitting it.
